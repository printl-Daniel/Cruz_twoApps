1. Driver Type

Dynamic Drivers: Most common; delivers powerful bass and loud sound.

Planar Magnetic Drivers: Known for superior sound quality and precision.

Electrostatic Drivers: Rare and expensive, providing high-end clarity and detail.

2. Frequency Response

Measured in Hertz (Hz), indicates the range of sounds the headphones can reproduce.

Typical range: 20Hz to 20kHz, with lower values representing bass and higher values treble.

3. Impedance

Measured in Ohms (Ω), affects the amount of power required to drive the headphones.

Low impedance (below 32Ω): Easier to power from phones and portable devices.

High impedance (above 100Ω): Requires more power, typically used with amplifiers.

4. Sensitivity

Measured in decibels (dB), determines how loud the headphones can get.

Higher sensitivity: Louder sound with less power.

5. Connectivity

Wired: Standard 3.5mm jack or USB connection for consistent sound quality.

Wireless: Bluetooth, often with aptX or AAC codecs for higher sound quality.

USB-C/Lightning: Used for devices without 3.5mm jacks.

6. Noise Cancellation

Active Noise Cancellation (ANC): Uses microphones to cancel out ambient noise.

Passive Noise Isolation: Achieved through design, blocking out noise physically.

7. Soundstage

The sense of space and depth in sound; important for immersive listening experiences.

8. Build Quality

Materials: Plastic for lightweight designs, metal for durability.

Ear Cups: Leather or fabric for comfort during long sessions.

9. Battery Life (Wireless)

Measured in hours, the longer the battery life, the better for wireless use.

Fast Charging: Some headphones offer quick charging features.

10. Controls

Touch Controls: Swipe and tap for volume and track control.

Physical Buttons: Traditional buttons for power, volume, and playback control.

11. Microphone

Built-in microphone for calls and voice assistants.

Noise-canceling microphones for clearer call quality.
